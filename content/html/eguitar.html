How an electric guitar works

Electric guitars are my passion; one of them, at least. I love their eclectic nature, ranging all the way from sensitive to energetic, from cristal clean to horridly noisy, from subtle to loud. They're my big frustration too: I've spent years on them, but achieved nothing worth. I love music, but I'm terribly bad at it.

Anyway, I like talking about guitars, hence this article. My intention isn't explaining the obvious: that an electric guitar works by converting the vibration of a steel-made string into an electric signal by electromagnetic induction (the same working principle of your bycicle dynamo). My goal is fully different. You often watch a guy playing a weathered guitar with a loosy amp and: wow, how could possibly this guy get that sound?! That's what I want to talk about.

And -well- it's mostly wave physics plus taste and musical sense. The first can be explained, that's what I'm in. The second is a mistery, one may have it or not. But it's the most important ingredient, and sadly the one I lack.

It's worth noting that wave physics also plays a big role in acoustic / classical guitars, of course, but its effect is highly emphasized in electric guitars by amplification and distortion.

Because -first important lesson here- the instrument of the electric guitarist is the combination of guitar and amplifier. Not only because an electric guitar is useless without an amp; also because a certain guitar and a certain amp defines what the player can do. Sound-wise, I mean; playing technique is a fully different matter.

Ok, let's play! I'm starting by purely-acoustic wave physics and then moving on to the effect of amplification on it.

1. Dynamics

The most obvious aspect of the sound coming from a string is not volume-related. The dynamics, or acoustic envelope, of a plucked string is something like this:

You see, there's a sharp initial raise (or attack), no sustain and a relatively quick decay that soon gets below your hearing threshold causing the string to "stop" ringing (although it may keep vibrating for a bit longer). This is quite straightforward to get even for people without a fine ear.

Not much more to say about that, except that the acoustic envelope is one of the two characteristics that make up for the timbre of an instrument, what tells apart a guitar from a flicorno, let's say.

2. One string vibrating - Harmonics

When you pluck a guitar string you intuitively guess that it's going to vibrate this way:

The resulting tone is directly related to the vibration frequency, and that depends of three physycal features of the string:

  • Length. The longer the basser. Bass guitars are longer than -well- regular guitars, i.e. Wave length is in fact the inverse of frequency.

  • Gauge. The heavier the basser. Again, bass guitars have heavier strings than guitars.

  • Tension. The higher the trebler. Within a guitar, strings have diminishing gauges to get higher tones while keeping a uniform tension; otherwise, treble strings would need higher tension (and would be harder to pluck).

In physycs these different modes of vibration are called -well- modes of vibration, but in musical theory they'are usually called harmonics. Four important things to know about them are:

  • Harmonic wave lentghs are always related to the furndamental (base) tone by simple fractions. In the pictures above you see wave lenghts of: 1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4... The related fequencies, and thus the sound tones, are the inverse of those: 1, 2, 3, 4.

  • There're infinite harmonics or increasing frequency, but

  • As you may see in the pictures above they're diminishing in amplitude, and thus sound volume. The fundamental is the louder harmonic, and higher ones are progressively quieter.

  • The most shocking fact: all harmonics are always present when a string is vibrating.

Yes, that's right. You need a fine ear to get them, but infinite harmonic overtones, with diminishing amplitude, are there whenever you pluck a guitar string.

In acoustic guitars, the string vibration propagates through the bridge to the guitar top. The top may react differently to different frequencies, and therefore some harmonics may be damped or even killed altogether. That's what distinguishes a classical guitar from a western acoustic, for instance (the later being richer in higher harmonics). In fact, harmonic spectrum is the second component of an instrument timbre.

You can also modify slightly the harmonic spectrum by retaining the string at certain points with your finger tip, but that's part of playing technique and not too relevant for our current discussion.

To end this section: as you may know, or suspect, harmonics are the base of the musical device of harmony. But that's too huge a world apart, we won't come into it (today).

3. Two (or more) strings vibrating - Wave addition?

When you have two random waves together you intuitively guess they will add to form a larger wave, but look:

When two peaks or two valleys meet they do add up; but when a peak meets a valley, they actually substract. When two random strings are plucked together what you got is an irregular, pulsating wave unpleasant to you ear. That's what dissonance is.

But it is actually worse. Remember harmonics? When two strings are plucked together what you got are two full sets of harmonics fighting each other, causing a mess of interfeering waves. Acostic chaos!

You may be thinking that when two strings in a guitar are plucked together they usually sound good. Well, you're right, but they do only under very restrictive conditions:

  • The guitar is properly tuned; and

  • You finger the right frets to choose two notes that actually fit together.

That right fit is achieved when both notes' fundamental frequencies have a simple mathematical relation such as: 1, 1/2, 1/3, 2/3... The reason is that, while they still will interfere with each other, they'll do in a regular, periodic manner that your ear will easilly accept. That's what we call a chord. We're being dragged again into the world of harmony, which is precisely the theory of chord formation; but still, we won't come into it (today).

Achieving a nice relation between two fundamental notes doesn't ensure that all of their harmonics will do. In fact most of the times they won't, but you know, higher harmonics have progressively diminishing volumes, so the interference they cause is progressively less relevant. That's why some kind of chords sound better than others; some are smooth and regular (i.e. major chords), some are slightly dissonant. You need a very fine ear to pick that; people not having it (like myself) can anyway perceive the subjective, distinct "mood" of each chord type. And anyway they can definitely learn harmony, duh.

Of course guitar players aren't restricted to two-note chords, up to six can be used, but that doesn't change the discussion above too much. Sure, the more notes the messier, but when you play six-string chords in a guitar you're most of the time playing simple 5th (three-note) chords with some duplicate notes. That's fine to gain volume and presence without extra harmonic complexity. Certain styles of music favour 7th and higher chords where a greater amount of dissonance is unavoidable; Jazz, for instance (no wonder dissonance is an important feature of Jazz music).

A last thing I should mention. What if you have two waves of the same frequency and amplitude playing together? If their peaks and valleys are "synchronized" they just add up, producing a wave two times the amplitude; in wave physics they're said to be in phase. When out of phase sometimes they'll add and sometimes they'll substract, therefore yielding a wave of the same frequency but with less-than-double amplitude.

When in anti-phase they will cancel each other altogether.

That can't happen within an acoustic guitar. But it'll be relevant later, and now was the right moment to introduce it.

Ok, time to switch the amp on.

4. Electric amplification - Distortion!

An amp, by definition, takes an electric signal and outputs a higher level one. The output / input level ratio is what we call the amp gain.

But every electronic amp has a maximum, or saturation, output level which can't be exceeded; when your input level times gain would be over the saturation level, what you got is exactly that level.

The fine thing about old-school tube amps favoured by guitar players is that the transition from gain operation to saturation is relatively smooth.

If you feed a weak input signal into the amp, it may keep within the gain zone. As a result you get a high-fidelity reproduction of your signal with higher volume level. It's when you feed a higher level signal, or use a higher gain, when the amp starts doing its magic.

You see, your clean sine wave is transformed into a weird shape, resulting from softly clipping the original peaks. That's what we call distortion, and when it makes its appearance beautiful things happen.

Take for instance your acoustic envelope; under distortion the string dynamics are transformed into something like this:

You're losing attack, gaining sustain and a longer decay. That makes up for the usual long-ringing, flute-like dynamics of a guitar solo.

Maybe even more dramatic is the effect on harmonics. Lower (and louder) harmonics may push the amp into saturation, while higher ones may still fit within the gain zone. As a result, the fundamental is equalized with adjacent harmonics and higher ones get to be more prominent, making up for a richer tone with a lot of low-end and sparkling top.

So, you're altering the dynamics and harmonic spectrum of your guitar, and therefore its timbre. You can have many different instruments just by changing your amp gain!

What if you pluck two strings simultaneously? Remember that harmonic interference will usually lead to a pulsating wave. That's barely noticeable in an acoustic guitar, but, as said before, distortion clips / equalizes lower harmonics and emphasizes the higher ones, making up for a lot of potential interference. As a result a chord in an electric guitar will result in a sustained, rasp, growling sound that makes your heart jump in wild emotion. Rock and Roll!

If you pluck more strings, you get into trouble, though. The more harmonics you add -and the more distortion you set- the more a harmonic mess your sound will be. When playing chords in an electric guitar you need to make a compromise: if you want complex harmonies you need to keep you amp gain low. If you want heavy distortion you need to keep chords to its bare minimum; that's why power chords where devised: simple two- o three-note chords that will sound neat and punchy under distortion.

As a conclussion, it must be emphasized that the main aspect governing amp distortion (apart from the amp gain) is input level. Low input levels will go neutral through your amp, only high-enough levels will push it into saturation. Between your guitar and your amp, a lot of things may modify your signal level, starting by the guitar and the player themselves: some guitars are "hotter" than others (see pickup sections below) and you may pluck the string softly or heavily.

Every effect pedal and effect processor is a point where signal level may change. Amplification-based (distortion) pedals and boosters, by definition, affect your signal level. You don't only need to care about the signal level fed into the amp but also the level that every pedal feeds into the next one. Of course every pedal has an output volume knob for that.

Time-based effects (phasers, choruses, flangers, delays) are specially tricky, as their output level will usually oscillate. Chances there are that the output signal from those saturate your amp only at periodic -or worse, erratic- peaks, leading your amp to a pulsating or unpredictable distortion.

5. Amp stages

In primitive amps distortion was deemed a deffect or limitation. The idea was to amplify the signal with high-fidelity, but with the technology then available some distortion couldn't be avoided when the amp was taken near its maximum volume.

Anyway, those first electric guitar players soon appreciated the benefits of distortion, and it became a prominent feature in Rock music. But then a problem showed up: in order to have distortion the amp needed to be cranked up, which could or couldn't be appropiate for certain gigs: given the required volume for the room size, a low power amp could give too much distortion and a high power one maybe too little.

That difficulty was overcame by building amps in two stages called preamp and power stage. In theory, the preamp is where the distortion is set, usually by a knob labelled gain. After that, the power stage sets the required ouput volume by a master knob and usually has a builtin equalizer to tweak your tone. The two-stage design has an additional benefit: some effect pedals and processors that are more suitable to be applied after the signal distortion can be inserted between both stages; amps usually have send / return jacks for that. Amps also usually feature several channels; that's simply different preamps with different amounts of distortion, suitable for different styles of music as well as to change dynamics within a piece (for example, between rythm and solo work). Most amps are built this way nowadays.

Anyway, in an all-tube amp, the power stage can distort too! And some players favour its usually harsh distortion over the creamier one produced by the preamp tubes. So, back to the beginning: the desired distortion amount and quality doesn't always relate well to the required output level. First consideration when buying an amp, therefore, is the required power for the player needs. Some modern amps feature selectable output level for the power stage; that is a wonderful idea.

6. Playing at high volume → Feedback!

Well, you have your string vibration transformed into an electric wave, sent to the amp, amplified and cast into the air by the loudspeaker. The pressure waves (that's what sound is) will get back to the string; when playing at high volume, chances there are that they push the string into vibration again, thus reinforcing the electric wave sent to the amp, pushing further the string and so on. You've hit a feedback loop!

If distortion makes your guitar get sustain, feedback can make for a lot more of it. Actually, given the right conditions, feedback can make your guitar sustain forever (while your amp keeps working, duh). Feedback also reinforces your input signal to the amp, pushing it further into saturation, thus enriching your tone.

The beautiful thing about feedback is its live, organic, wild nature. For it to work, the right harmonic need to reach the string in phase, and that's not easy. Sound waves are reflected on walls and other objects; a certain harmonic may be filtered out by the reflecting surface material, and those which make up to the string will hit it in or out of phase depending on the overall round trip distance from the loudspeaker to the reflecting surface and back to the string. Hence, feedback is really sensitive to the environment, amp equalization, body position and even room temperature. Controlling it is part of the playing technique of an electric guitar player.

Sure, you crank up your amp master volume knob and eventually any string will get into feedback, but it's a miracle it'll have any musical value. For it to add up to your music you need to get the right harmonic in the right amount at the precise moment you need it. That's tough to master.

In any case you need to play your guitar LOUD. Not as loud as to make your head explode or push your amp into an uncontrollable mess, but in the right amount to achieve some tone-improving feedback. It's a shame that's usually above the tolerable volume level for your home. If you're wondering whether your amp isn't worth the lot of money you paid for it, maybe you just need to crank it up a little bit. Get a rehearsal room. Use taste.

7. Pickups

Pickups, you know, are those little devices below the strings where the electromagnetic induction thing happens.

First thing to know is that there're two fundamental ways to build a pickup: with a single coil or with two reversely-magnetized coils.

Single-coil pickup are the simplest and oldest ones. They provide a clean, harmonic-rich, bell-like sound, somehow similar to that of an acoustic guitar. They have a problem, too: they're very prone to pick up enviromental electromagnetic radiation (tipically from electric apparels nearby) causing an audible hum in your amp that gets to be very annoying at high gains and high volumes.

That's precisely what two-reversely-magnetized-coils pickups where designed for, and that's why they were given the catchier denomination of humbuckers. These pickups effectively cancel environment-inducted hum, but at the cost of a poorer tone: darker, heavier, with less harmonics.

Both types are in use nowadays, and are usually devoted to different kinds of music. Single-coils are appropiate for light, low-distortion sounds, where hum isn't much of a problem and harmonic spectrum isn't too much adulterated. Humbuckers, on the other side, perform better with high distortion because their narrower spectrum prevents the messy harmonic interference to some extent. Also because a pair of coils give a higher signal that pushes your amp into saturation more readily.

Electric guitars usually have two or three pickups in different positions usually identified as: neck, middle (when there's one) and bridge pickup. The main difference between them is that they see the string differently. The neck pickup is near (or at) the center of the string, therefore it picks up the first (fundamental) harmonic at its maximum amplitude; bridge pickup, on the other side gets very little of that and so its sound is relatively poor in low harmonics.

As a result, the neck pickup yields a mellow, complex sound while that of the bridge pickup is brighter and more articulate. Usually the first is preferred for rythm work and the later for soloing, but of course anyone can use them at will.

I'm not forgetting about the middle pickup: it just has an intermediate character between the two others, so no much to say about it. Usually a certain guitar will feature all single-coils or all humbuckers, although mixed sets aren't uncommon-

The switch for pickup selection will usually allow to choose between the two or three available and several combinations of them:

  • In a two-pickup guitar a three-way switch allows for neck, neck+bridge or bridge pickup operation.

  • In a three-pickup guitar a five-way switch allows for neck, neck+middle, middle, middle+bridge or bridge pickup operation.

You may guess that combining two pickups will yield a sound that's a mix of both. You're right, but there're still some considerations:

  • There're two essential ways of combining two pickups: in series or in parallel. The later is the preferred method for a simple reason: in-series pickups double the signal, so changing from single to combined pickups would require adjusting your volume knob to keep a consistent signal level; that's difficult to manage while playing. But

  • When connecting pickups in series and anti-phase an interesting effect shows up. Two adjacents pickups will see pretty much the same fundamental but different amounts of higher armonics. Thus, in anti-phase the fundamental is mostly cancelled out, leaving only higher harmonics and resulting in a harsh, bright sound. Generally speaking, this feature is only found in custom guitars specially tailored for a player's needs; those may also include an in-phase / anti-phase switch to select the mode of operation (although in-series and in-phase pickup pairs are difficult to handle, as said before).

  • In guitars equipped with single-coil pickups, they're usually installed with their magnets alternated. That way, when you combine two adjacent pickups they will benefit from some humbucking effect. For the hum-canceling part, at least: real humbuckers usually have their coils connected in series, thus yielding a higher level signal too.

  • Another interesting tweak is having coil-splittable humbuckers: specially wired humbuckers allowing for switcheable single / dual coil operation.

8. Equalization

As said before, mostly every amp has a builtin equalizer (eq for short) that serves a few different purposes. They're usually two- (bass, treble) or three-band (bass, mid, treble); if in need of a finer tweaking you can always insert an external multi-band eq betwen the preamp and the power stage. If you're really picky, another eq can be inserted before the preamp to tweak the signal you feed into it.

You may guess what's the main purpose of a guitar amp eq: setting your overall tone to cut through the band mix. The natural place for the guitar within a band is in the mids, so you'll usually set a lot of mid and relatively low bass and treble. The exactly opposite equalization isn't uncommon, though: high bass, high treble and low mid: along with heavy distortion, this "scoop eq" yields a dark, heavy sound with rasp highs, usually found in some metal styles. It can also be nice when you play by yourself, as it makes your guitar sound wider and fill the space.

Having a builtin eq in the amp is truly a need because, you know, your gain setting alters your tone a lot. Adding gain results in a darker tone with somehow aggresive highs that you'll need to trim to your band mix. That's why a good amp won't have just one but a dedicated eq for every preamp channel.

The eq is also important to favour feedback where you want it to be (tonally speaking) and restraining your pickup hum, if it's there.

Of course you can't always have all that said above, so equalization is usually a matter of compromise. Tweak it to taste.

A more general use for the eq, not only related to guitar amps but to every sound system, is profiling your sound to compensate for room behaviour. A certain wave going off the speaker will expand to every surface in the room; depending on the wave frequency and the surface material, it may rebound cleanly, damped or either be absorbed. Thus the wave will reach your ear directly from the speaker and rebounded from surfaces nearby in different amplitude and phases, which may therefore add or substract. As a result, the room you're in, and your position within it, will favour some frequencies and damage others, severly affecting the sound spectrum. That's the main reason why every decent sound system needs an eq.

If you're playing by yourself at home, you can tweak your amp for that, but playing for an audience is a fully different matter: you can set a sound that makes you happy in your playing position, but everybody else in the room will pick a different one. That's why achieving a good live sound is so hard (and in certain rooms not even possible). In big gigs, the sound coming off the amp is mic'ed (picked up by a microphone) and sent to a PA, where a sound technician can work out a good overall mix. The mic is usually placed very close to the amp speaker, almost touching the cover grid, in order to get only the sound coming directly off it, and in an very specific position relative to the speaker cone.
